# Polymarket Rebuilt Its Exchange Stack This Morning. The Order-Book Wipe Is the Smallest Change.

At 11:00 UTC today, Polymarket paused trading for about an hour, executed a coordinated cutover from CTF Exchange V1 to V2, and replaced its bridged USDC.e collateral with a Polymarket-issued stablecoin. Every resting limit order on the platform was cancelled in the process. By 12:00 UTC the new stack was live, wallets were reconnected, and traders began re-quoting against a different book — same markets, different rails.

Most coverage will frame this as a maintenance window. It is not. It is the first time Polymarket has owned its full vertical stack — collateral, matching engine, attribution, wallet compatibility — without depending on a bridged asset or a third-party order-book contract.

## The collateral swap

The headline piece is **pUSD**, a Polymarket-issued ERC-20 on Polygon that replaced USDC.e as platform collateral. It is backed 1:1 by USDC, with the redemption guarantee enforced on-chain by the contract itself rather than off-chain attestations. Existing balances were swept to pUSD via a single front-end approval; for users without the front-end, a manual `wrap()` call on the Collateral Onramp contract was required.

The structural read: Polymarket stops being a tenant of Circle's bridge. The company controls minting, redemption, and the on-chain reserve check directly. With Polymarket's chain-migration discussion live for several weeks — Solana, Sui, and a possible POLY-branded L2 are all on the candidate list — owning the collateral token is the prerequisite that lets them lift the rest of the platform off Polygon when they choose. The migration question stops being "can we leave?" and becomes "where do we go?"

## A new matching engine, with on-chain attribution

CTF Exchange V2 is not a fork of V1. It is a rewritten order matcher, audited by Cantina and Quantstamp, with three changes that matter for active traders.

First, **fees are calculated at match time** rather than at order-placement time. The V1 design occasionally rejected matches when post-placement fee state diverged from order state — V2 eliminates that class of failure by deferring the calculation. Quieter than it sounds: nonce invalidations and balance-check race conditions, the recurring ghost in V1's matching engine, are gone with it.

Second, the contract added **EIP-1271 signature verification**. This is what allows smart-contract wallets and account-abstraction stacks to sign orders directly. Browser-extension wallets were not the bottleneck for institutional flow; signature compatibility was. That gate is now open, and the door it opens leads to custodied flow that will not touch a MetaMask popup.

Third, builder codes — the attribution mechanism by which third-party clients earn rebates on the volume they route — moved from off-chain bookkeeping into a **native `builderCode` field on every order**. Attribution becomes a property of the order itself, settled automatically. For builders running on the platform, this is the difference between trusting a back-office spreadsheet and reading directly from the chain.

## What broke, and what didn't

**Cancelled:** every limit order on the V1 book. Polymarket's documentation flagged this for a week; the operator-side reaction in market chat did not. Expect a 24–48 hour re-quoting window where spreads on lower-volume binaries widen until market makers refill the book. Public-facing chart data showed the artifact in real time — several markets briefly snapped to 50% mid-cutover before reverting on the next data tick.

**Carried over:** active market positions, user balances (now denominated in pUSD), market resolution clocks, and any open trade on a market that has not yet expired. No one lost a settled outcome; they lost a place in the queue.

**Required action:** API users had to switch to V2 endpoints and update the `CLOB-Client` SDK in TypeScript, Python, or Go. V1 client libraries are not forward-compatible, and a bug-bounty program went live alongside the audited contracts.

## The bigger pattern

Polymarket has spent April rebuilding the parts of its stack that depended on third parties. Crypto perps shipped on April 21 — up to 10x leverage on BTC, NVDA, and gold. Kalshi answered with onshore perps the same week. pUSD-only collateral shipped today. The chain migration is now technically possible; the only thing left to decide is which chain.

Read against the four-way structural fight with Kalshi, FanDuel-Predicts, and CME for US event-betting flow, the cutover is the foundation move that makes every other vertical-integration decision cheap to execute later. Custodied institutional flow needed EIP-1271. A platform-controlled stablecoin needed the V2 contracts. A chain migration needs collateral that does not live on the chain you are leaving. All three landed in a single hour-long window this morning.

The order-book wipe was the visible part. The reason the wipe was acceptable is the rest of the change.
