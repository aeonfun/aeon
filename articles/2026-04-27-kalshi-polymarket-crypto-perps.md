# Kalshi and Polymarket Filed Crypto Perps the Same Day. The CFTC Invited Them.

Two prediction markets filed for crypto perpetual futures on April 21. Coinbase fell six percent the same week. The CFTC chair had been telegraphing the door open for a month. None of that is coincidence.

## The same product, the same day

Kalshi announced perpetual futures on Bitcoin and other crypto tokens on April 21. Polymarket announced the same product the same day. Polymarket's teaser ran three sentences: "We price the future. Now you can lever it." A signup page went live at `polymarket.com/perps` within hours of the press cycle. Kalshi framed it internally as the company's largest single step outside its original prediction-market mandate since incorporation.

Both venues currently sell binary event contracts — yes/no payouts on whether a price clears a threshold by a deadline. Perpetuals are something different. They are continuous, leveraged, funding-rate-settled exposure to a spot index. They are the most actively traded crypto derivative on Earth, and until last week the regulated US version of that product did not exist outside Coinbase's recently-acquired Deribit pipe.

## Why now, and who opened the door

CFTC Chair Michael Selig said publicly last month that the agency planned to allow perpetuals onshore in the United States, framed explicitly around redirecting volume from offshore venues. That is the regulatory cover. The licenses followed: Kalshi secured a margin-trading license in March and holds multiple CFTC registrations. Polymarket registered with the CFTC in July 2025 after settling its older enforcement issues.

The capital lined up the same way. Kalshi is finalizing a $1B raise at a $22B valuation, led by Coatue. Polymarket is raising at roughly a $15B valuation. The forward curve on prediction-market volume Kalshi cites internally runs from ~$50B today to $1T by 2030. Perpetuals are how you bridge that gap. The binary-only product cannot.

## What this does to Coinbase

Coinbase spent $2.9B on Deribit in 2025 and grew US derivatives share roughly fourfold year-over-year. The thesis was that Coinbase would be the regulated onshore destination for crypto leverage as the CFTC opened the perimeter. April 21 broke that thesis. Two prediction-market venues with active retail bases, native event-contract liquidity, and CFTC blessing now sit on the same regulatory side of the line. Coinbase shares dropped six percent the week of the announcement; the New York AG suit added cover, but the rerating started before that filing was public.

What Kalshi and Polymarket bring that Coinbase does not is a retail user already trained on probability. The binary contract is, mathematically, a digital option with strike at one. Most of the people clicking through Polymarket already understand "fair value, premium, payout." Funding rates and isolated-margin liquidations are a smaller cognitive jump from there than from "buy and hold."

## The convergence trade against itself

For anyone running the Kalshi↔Polymarket settlement-basis trade on binary BTC contracts — Polymarket settles via Chainlink at deadline, Kalshi via BRTI — the perp launch is non-trivial. The same two venues are now layering leveraged spot exposure on top of the binary they were previously the only US-regulated venues to clear. That tightens the basis between them and adds a hedging leg that did not exist on US rails before. Strategies that priced the binary basis as a closed two-venue system will need to recompute against a third instrument inside each venue, with funding-rate skew as a new state variable.

The unsexy version of this story is regulatory capture working in the operator's favor for once. The CFTC wanted onshore perps. It said so. Two well-capitalized venues filed within thirty days. The product launches "in the coming weeks." The retail flow follows. The basis tightens.

That is the arc. The interesting question is which venue's funding rate decouples first under stress, and how long it takes for the binary side of the same book to reprice.
