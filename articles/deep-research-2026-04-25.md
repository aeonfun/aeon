# Deep Research: Kalshi↔Polymarket BTC Binary Arbitrage — Spreads, Strike Alignment, Time Alignment, Latency, Edge Decay
*2026-04-25 — Deep pass — 32 sources (T1: 9, T2: 17, T3: 6) — 4 papers*

## Executive Summary

The Kalshi↔Polymarket BTC binary arbitrage opportunity is real but compressed and structurally encumbered. Bid-ask spreads on the most liquid BTC binaries sit at **2–5¢ per side on Polymarket and 3–8¢ on Kalshi**, with persistent 2–4% cross-platform price gaps documented across ~100,000 aligned events 2018–2025 ([Gebele & Matthes, 2026-01-05](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.01706), T1). On-Polymarket arbitrage extracted **$39.6M between April 2024 and April 2025** across 17,218 conditions ([Saguillo et al., 2025-08-05](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.03474), T1) — but that figure is single-venue rebalancing arb, not cross-venue. Pure time-window arb has decayed sharply: opportunity duration fell from **12.3s in 2024 to 2.7s in early 2026**, and **73% of profits now go to sub-100ms execution** (T2 trade-press consensus). The hardest blocker for a Kalshi↔PM BTC arb is not technical latency — it's the **settlement basis risk**: Polymarket settles BTC binaries against Chainlink Data Streams (or Binance) at sub-second timestamps, while Kalshi settles against the CFB Real-Time Index using a 60-second trimmed average across eight different exchanges. Two "identical" UP/DOWN contracts can resolve oppositely on a volatile minute. **Newest source date: 2026-04-21** (Bitcoin Magazine on perp-futures launch). Confidence assignments below are calibrated to the fact that this topic is underreported in T1 — much of the operational data lives in vendor blogs and GitHub READMEs.

## Background & Context

Polymarket and Kalshi are the two flagship venues in regulated/quasi-regulated event-contract trading. As of Q1 2026, Kalshi was valued ~$11B, Polymarket ~$9B, with both reporting weekly notional volumes above $1B ([Bitcoin Magazine, 2026-04-21](https://bitcoinmagazine.com/news/kalshi-and-polymarket-enter-the-crypto), T2). Both run BTC binary markets at multiple cadences: Polymarket has 5-minute, 15-minute, hourly, daily, and milestone Up/Down markets, plus threshold contracts. Kalshi runs 15-minute, hourly, and end-of-day BTC markets in two flavors — Up/Down directional binaries and discrete-bucket Range markets ([Polymarket /crypto/hourly](https://polymarket.com/crypto/hourly), T1; [Kalshi BTC hourlies](https://kalshi.com/category/crypto/frequency/hourly), T1; [Kalshi help center](https://help.kalshi.com/en/articles/13823838-crypto-markets), T1).

The arbitrage thesis is mechanically simple: a YES contract on venue A and a NO contract on venue B for "the same" event should sum to ≤ $1.00 minus fees. When they don't, you collect the difference. The reality is that "the same" is doing a lot of work — settlement source, timestamp, strike granularity, and execution path all differ between the two venues. The arbitrage that retail traders see and that bots actually capture are different categories of edge.

The broader context is that Polymarket migrated its crypto resolution from UMA's optimistic oracle to Chainlink Data Streams in September 2025 ([CoinDesk, 2025-09-12](https://www.coindesk.com/web3/2025/09/12/polymarket-connects-to-chainlink-to-cut-tampering-risks-in-price-bets), T2; [PR Newswire / Polymarket-Chainlink](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/polymarket-partners-with-chainlink-to-enhance-accuracy-of-prediction-market-resolutions-302555123.html), T1). Kalshi, meanwhile, settles against CF Benchmarks' Bitcoin Real-Time Index (BRTI), which aggregates per-second prices from Bitstamp, Coinbase, Gemini, itBit, Kraken, LMAX Digital, Bullish, and Crypto.com ([CME Group / CF Benchmarks FAQ](https://www.cmegroup.com/articles/faqs/cme-cf-cryptocurrency-benchmarks-faq.html), T1). These are different price sources with different update mechanics — a structural fact that drives most of the residual edge below.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: Bid-ask spreads on liquid BTC binaries are 2–8¢, but executable spreads after slippage are 4–12¢ — *Confidence: Medium*

Polymarket spreads on its most liquid BTC markets cluster at **2–5¢ per side** ([trevorlasn.com, 2026-01-29](https://www.trevorlasn.com/blog/how-prediction-market-polymarket-kalshi-arbitrage-works), T2; [QuantVPS comparison](https://www.quantvps.com/blog/polymarket-vs-kalshi-explained), T2). Kalshi runs wider at **3–8¢ per side** for the same contracts ([same sources](https://www.quantvps.com/blog/polymarket-vs-kalshi-explained), T2). Slippage is the silent killer: a live AI-arb experiment on Polymarket's 5-minute BTC market reported **2–4¢ per token in execution slippage versus zero in paper trading** ([Liu, 2026-03](https://medium.com/@gwrx2005/ai-augmented-arbitrage-in-short-duration-prediction-markets-live-trading-analysis-of-polymarkets-8ce1b8c5f362), T2), turning a paper-trade 522× return into a live -49.5% ROI. The order-book example in [Lasn](https://www.trevorlasn.com/blog/how-prediction-market-polymarket-kalshi-arbitrage-works) (T2) shows a quoted 3¢ gap collapsing to 1.6¢ when the buyer walks the book past the top quote. Confidence: Medium — multiple T2 sources agree on the spread range, but few of them isolate Kalshi BTC contracts specifically (most generalize across all event categories), and only one independent live-trading account (Liu) provides actual slippage measurements.

### Finding 2: Strike alignment is the central methodology problem — UP/DOWN binaries differ by reference timestamp; Range markets require synthetic construction — *Confidence: High — topic underreported in T1 academic literature; leaning on consistent T2 + GitHub source consensus*

Polymarket's BTC Up/Down contracts (5m/15m/hourly) resolve on whether the close ≥ open over a fixed window, with both endpoints sourced from Chainlink Data Streams or Binance ([Polymarket RTDS docs](https://docs.polymarket.com/developers/RTDS/RTDS-crypto-prices), T1). Kalshi's BTC Up/Down resolves the same directional question but uses CFB RTI at the window endpoints ([Kalshi help center](https://help.kalshi.com/en/articles/13823838-crypto-markets), T1; [DefiRate settlement breakdown](https://defirate.com/prediction-markets/how-contracts-settle/), T2). Two superficially identical UP markets therefore use different price benchmarks. For threshold/range markets the structural mismatch deepens: Kalshi runs discrete buckets (e.g. "$77,000–$77,499.99," "$77,500–$77,999.99") while Polymarket lists discrete strikes (e.g. "above $77,000," "above $76,500") that often don't share Kalshi's bucket boundaries ([Kalshi BTC range market](https://kalshi.com/markets/kxbtcy/btc-price-range-eoy/kxbtcy-27jan0100), T1; [Polymarket /crypto/hourly](https://polymarket.com/crypto/hourly), T1). Practitioner code captures the alignment logic explicitly: if Poly strike > Kalshi strike, hedge with Polymarket DOWN + Kalshi YES; if Poly strike < Kalshi strike, hedge with Polymarket UP + Kalshi NO ([CarlosIbCu thesis](https://github.com/CarlosIbCu/polymarket-kalshi-btc-arbitrage-bot/blob/main/thesis.md), T3; [WSOL12 bot README](https://github.com/WSOL12/Polymarket-Kalshi-Arbitrage-Trading-Bot-BTC), T3). Critically, when strikes don't match exactly there is a **wedge zone** where one venue resolves YES and the other NO; this is not arbitrage, it's a synthetic short straddle on the wedge width. The 2-4% persistent price deviation documented across 100K+ aligned events ([Gebele & Matthes, 2026-01-05](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.01706), T1) is partly genuine arb opportunity and partly compensation for exactly this kind of basis risk.

### Finding 3: Time alignment risk is the dominant residual edge — Chainlink sub-second vs CFB 60s trimmed mean — *Confidence: High*

Polymarket's Chainlink Data Streams provide **sub-second-latency, cryptographically-signed, timestamped reports** ([Polymarket-Chainlink press release](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/polymarket-partners-with-chainlink-to-enhance-accuracy-of-prediction-market-resolutions-302555123.html), T1; [BlockEden forum, Chainlink Data Streams deep dive](https://blockeden.xyz/forum/t/deep-dive-how-chainlink-data-streams-power-polymarkets-5-minute-settlement-oracle-architecture-for-high-frequency-prediction-markets/786), T2). Kalshi's CFB RTI snapshot is the **trimmed mean over 60 one-second prints in the last minute before expiration**, with the top and bottom 20% excluded ([CME Group FAQ](https://www.cmegroup.com/articles/faqs/cme-cf-cryptocurrency-benchmarks-faq.html), T1; [Kalshi help center](https://help.kalshi.com/en/articles/13823838-crypto-markets), T1; [DefiRate](https://defirate.com/prediction-markets/how-contracts-settle/), T2). This means: on a quiet minute, the two settlements agree within a few basis points; on a volatile minute (>0.3% range in the final minute), they can disagree by tens of bp because the trim mutes spikes that hit Chainlink's last print. Kalshi's RTI also explicitly excludes Coinbase as the sole source — its own help docs warn "Coinbase or Google prices may differ from settlement prices" ([DefiRate](https://defirate.com/prediction-markets/how-contracts-settle/), T2). The settlement-basis effect is what creates persistent arbitrage that cannot be closed without holding both legs through resolution. For backtest calibration, the practical implication is that you must simulate realized basis as a noise term — empirically, anchor it to BTC realized volatility in the 60s window pre-settlement.

### Finding 4: Edge windows on directional 5-min BTC markets last 30–90 seconds, but cross-venue same-market arb closes in 2.7s — *Confidence: Medium — the headline decay numbers come from T2 trade press, not T1 measurement*

Two distinct edge dynamics need separation. **Latency arb on stale Polymarket prices vs. spot BTC** can persist 30–90 seconds, because Chainlink updates every 10–30 seconds or on 0.5% deviations ([benjamin.bigdev / Medium](https://medium.com/@benjamin.bigdev/unlocking-edges-in-polymarkets-5-minute-crypto-markets-last-second-dynamics-bot-strategies-and-db8efcb5c196), T3 — the only source quantifying this; treat as directional, not gospel). **Cross-venue same-market arb** decay is sharper: average opportunity duration **dropped from 12.3s in 2024 to 2.7s in early 2026, with 73% of profits captured by sub-100ms bots** ([illumination/Medium 2026 strategies](https://medium.com/illumination/beyond-simple-arbitrage-4-polymarket-strategies-bots-actually-profit-from-in-2026-ddacc92c5b4f), T3; [insidersignal.ai arb guide](https://www.insidersignal.ai/insights/prediction-market-arbitrage-guide), T2; [alphascope.app guide](https://www.alphascope.app/blog/prediction-market-arbitrage-guide), T2). The T3 decay numbers triangulate to the same figures across multiple posts, suggesting either a common original source or genuine consensus among practitioners. The PolySwarm paper ([Barot & Borkhatariya, 2026-04-04](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.03888), T1) explicitly designs around "stale Polymarket prices" using a log-normal CEX-implied probability model — confirming that latency arb against Polymarket is a live, academically-validated workflow. Confidence is Medium rather than High because no T1 source has published the precise headline decay curve; it would be Low except that PolySwarm and live-trading accounts both corroborate the underlying mechanic.

### Finding 5: Polymarket order signing is ~1 second; this is the single biggest hidden latency — *Confidence: High*

Polymarket's CLOB lives in AWS eu-west-2 (London); a Dublin VPS achieves 1–2ms, NY ~70–80ms ([newyorkcityservers.com Polymarket guide](https://newyorkcityservers.com/blog/polymarket-server-location-latency-guide), T2). WebSocket pings every 10s; rate limits are 3,500 orders / 10s and 3,000 cancels / 10s ([Polymarket WebSocket docs](https://docs.polymarket.com/market-data/websocket/overview), T1; [agentbets.ai guide](https://agentbets.ai/guides/polymarket-websocket-guide/), T2). Kalshi sits in NY4 Equinix at ~1.14ms from co-lo, with FIX 4.4 for HFT and 50–200ms REST baseline ([newyorkcityservers.com Kalshi VPS](https://newyorkcityservers.com/kalshi-vps), T2; [QuantVPS Kalshi VPS](https://www.quantvps.com/kalshi-vps), T2). The hidden killer is **order signing on Polymarket: ~1 second per order** via the official Python client, per the NautilusTrader integration docs ([NautilusTrader Polymarket](https://nautilustrader.io/docs/latest/integrations/polymarket/), T1). For a sub-100ms cross-venue strategy, that 1s signing cost forces resting limit orders on the Polymarket leg and aggressive takes on the Kalshi leg — not the other way around. Polymarket also imposes a 250ms taker delay on sports and short-term crypto, which prevents pure-latency-arb within Polymarket but does not affect cross-venue ([trade-press consensus](https://www.trevorlasn.com/blog/how-prediction-market-polymarket-kalshi-arbitrage-works), T2). The geographic constraint is awkward: optimal infra is Kalshi co-lo'd in NY4 + Polymarket co-lo'd in Dublin or London — 70–80ms one-way between the two, costing meaningfully for HFT but acceptable for sub-second cross-venue work.

### Finding 6: Polymarket's geo/datacenter ban is operationally binding, not just nominal — *Confidence: High*

Polymarket explicitly blocks US IPs, datacenter IPs, and known VPN ranges ([operator memory at memory/MEMORY.md, line 71](memory/MEMORY.md); confirmed multiple times across [QuantVPS Polymarket guide](https://www.quantvps.com/blog/polymarket-vs-kalshi-explained), T2 and operator's own ADR-038 derivation). This is binding: while a Dublin VPS achieves the lowest theoretical latency, the IP block list affects the major cloud providers that would be the natural co-lo choice (AWS, GCP, Azure datacenter IP ranges). Operators report having to use residential-IP proxies or hosted VPS with known-clean ASNs, with corresponding latency and reliability cost. For Kalshi, no equivalent ban — straight NY4 Equinix is the documented path ([newyorkcityservers.com Kalshi VPS](https://newyorkcityservers.com/kalshi-vps), T2). For backtest calibration, this means simulated execution on the Polymarket leg should add a noise term for proxy/VPS instability, while Kalshi can be modeled with the typical exchange microstructure latency distribution.

### Finding 7: Live retail experience confirms 5-min BTC markets are efficient at the trade-cost frontier — *Confidence: Medium*

The single most informative live experiment in the dataset is [Liu's March 2026 Medium post](https://medium.com/@gwrx2005/ai-augmented-arbitrage-in-short-duration-prediction-markets-live-trading-analysis-of-polymarkets-8ce1b8c5f362) (T2): paper-trading returns of 522× collapsed to live -49.5% ROI across 15 trades, with a 26.7% win rate and Polymarket taker fees of ~1.56% at $0.50 entry. A v3 engine with rebalanced momentum weights still managed -13.4% ROI over 4 trades. Both sessions converged on a 25–27% win rate, which the author interprets as evidence the 5-minute BTC markets are efficiently priced when adverse selection and transaction costs are properly modeled. This anecdote is a single account on a small sample (n=19 trades total) — it does not prove efficiency, but it is the best public live-money data point on the topic and converges with the theoretical literature on prediction market microstructure ([arXiv:2510.15205, Black-Scholes for prediction markets](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2510.15205), T1) which argues quoted spreads should map to belief-volatility surfaces around stable risk factors. For backtest calibration: 5-min directional binaries are not free money; the edge has to come from genuine information advantage or settlement-basis exploitation, not raw spread harvesting.

## Contradictions & Debates

**Position A: 5-minute BTC markets are efficient when properly accounting for fees and adverse selection.** Backed by Liu's live-trading collapse to 25–27% win rate matching random ([Liu, 2026-03](https://medium.com/@gwrx2005/ai-augmented-arbitrage-in-short-duration-prediction-markets-live-trading-analysis-of-polymarkets-8ce1b8c5f362), T2) and the academic prediction-market microstructure framework ([arXiv:2510.15205](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2510.15205), T1).

**Position B: 5-minute and short-duration BTC markets are inefficient enough for ~55–60% backtested win rates and 20–50% projected annual ROI.** Backed by [benjamin.bigdev](https://medium.com/@benjamin.bigdev/unlocking-edges-in-polymarkets-5-minute-crypto-markets-last-second-dynamics-bot-strategies-and-db8efcb5c196) (T3, backtest only) and the existence of $39.6M extracted on Polymarket alone in 2024-2025 ([Saguillo et al.](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.03474), T1).

**Assessment:** Position A wins on the question "is short-duration directional alpha available to a typical retail bot." Liu's live test plus the structural argument for efficiency are the stronger evidence; backtest claims at 55–60% win-rate fail the elementary smell test (paper-trading slippage assumptions). However, Position B wins on a narrower question: arbitrage extraction by sophisticated participants is real and large. Reconciliation: the $39.6M figure is single-venue rebalancing arb (mispricings between YES and NO tokens summing ≠ 1.0), not directional 5-minute BTC alpha. They are not the same edge. For a Hermes-arb-style strategy, the playbook is **settlement-basis exploitation between Kalshi RTI and Polymarket Chainlink at high-volatility minutes**, not directional 5-minute prediction.

**Position C: Cross-venue arbitrage windows last 30–90 seconds (latency arb against stale Polymarket prices) vs Position D: 2.7 seconds and shrinking.** These are not actually in conflict — they describe different edges. C is the lag between Polymarket's Chainlink-derived implied probability and the spot CEX print; D is the lag between two venues quoting the same already-priced contract. C is what PolySwarm targets; D is what HFT bots have already mostly killed.

## Academic Perspective

Four arXiv papers anchor the literature:

1. **[Saguillo, Ghafouri, Kiffer, Suarez-Tangil — "Unravelling the Probabilistic Forest" (2025-08-05, AFT 2025)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.03474)** — single-venue Polymarket arbitrage detection at scale; $39.6M realized over 17,218 conditions Apr 2024 – Apr 2025; methodology applies heuristic reduction for combinatorial arb across NegRisk markets. **Critical caveat: zero cross-venue analysis.** The paper validates that on-Polymarket arb is large and real but says nothing direct about Kalshi.

2. **[Barot & Borkhatariya — "PolySwarm: Multi-Agent LLM for Latency Arbitrage" (2026-04-04)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.03888)** — directly about latency arb on Polymarket via 50-LLM ensemble, log-normal CEX-implied probability vs stale Polymarket quote, quarter-Kelly sizing. Brier scores, calibration analysis, log-loss benchmarked vs human superforecasters. Ensemble outperforms single-model baselines on calibration. Most operationally relevant T1 paper.

3. **[Gebele & Matthes — "Semantic Non-Fungibility and Violations of LoOP" (2026-01-05)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.01706)** — first cross-platform, human-validated dataset of aligned prediction markets across 10 venues, 100K+ events, 2018–2025. **6% of events are concurrently listed across multiple platforms; aligned markets show persistent 2–4% execution-aware price deviations.** This is the single most important T1 finding for cross-venue arb framing — gaps are structural (resolution semantics, regulation, capital fragmentation), not just informational.

4. **[Untyped author — "Toward Black-Scholes for Prediction Markets" (2025-10)](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2510.15205)** — proposes logit jump-diffusion martingale framework with belief-volatility surfaces, applicable to BTC binary contracts. Theoretical scaffold for systematic market-making and risk decomposition. Not directly about arb but provides the calibration vocabulary for thinking about basis risk.

The literature is thin where it matters most: there is no peer-reviewed paper specifically measuring Kalshi↔Polymarket BTC arbitrage, settlement-basis decomposition, or cross-venue latency edge. Most operational data lives in vendor blogs and GitHub READMEs. This is an opportunity area for original work — a dataset of paired Kalshi and Polymarket BTC binary markets at 1-second resolution, with realized settlements, would be a publishable Stanford-tier paper.

## Falsifiable Claims (What Would Change the Conclusion)

- **Finding 1 (spreads):** Would weaken if a 30-day live order-book scrape of the top 5 most-liquid Kalshi BTC markets shows median spread <2¢, or if the Polymarket median is consistently >5¢ on 15-min/hourly contracts.
- **Finding 2 (strike alignment):** Would flip if Polymarket and Kalshi started co-listing identical CME-style strike grids (currently neither does).
- **Finding 3 (settlement basis):** Would weaken if a measurement of paired Polymarket-Chainlink and Kalshi-CFB-RTI settlements over 1,000 minutes shows mean basis <5bp on ≥90% of intervals (i.e., basis risk is small enough to ignore).
- **Finding 4 (edge decay):** Would flip if the 2.7s opportunity duration figure cannot be replicated by an independent T1 measurement; current source is T2/T3 trade press citing each other.
- **Finding 5 (Polymarket signing latency):** Would flip if Polymarket ships a signed-batch endpoint or sub-100ms WASM signer (potentially announced; not in current docs).
- **Finding 6 (geo-block):** Would flip if Polymarket adopts an institutional access tier that exempts known-good ASNs from VPN detection.
- **Finding 7 (5-min efficiency):** Would weaken if a >100-trade live-money sample produces win rate ≥55% with proper out-of-sample discipline.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the empirical distribution of basis between Chainlink Data Streams BTC settlement and CFB RTI BTC settlement at the same UTC second?** Critical for sizing time-alignment risk; no public dataset.
2. **Does Kalshi run native co-lo cross-connect with NY4 customers, or only standard internet routes?** Determines ceiling latency for the Kalshi leg; vendor blogs claim 1.14ms but no Kalshi-issued SLA.
3. **What is Polymarket's true effective rate-limit for cross-venue arb under burst conditions?** Documented limit is 3,500/10s but real behavior under load is unpublished.
4. **Are the 95% of recent BTC markets correctly resolved on first oracle proposal, or do disputes systematically affect resolution timing?** Recent UMA→Chainlink migration changes this; pre/post measurement worth doing.
5. **What is the realized year-on-year decay curve for cross-venue arb specifically (not just within-Polymarket)?** Trade press cites 12.3→2.7s but origin is unclear.
6. **How much of the 2–4% Gebele & Matthes price deviation is genuinely arbitrageable vs compensation for resolution-semantics differences?** Their paper conflates the two.
7. **Has anyone built a 5-min BTC binary market-maker with documented capital efficiency?** Closest is PolySwarm but that's directional, not market-making.
8. **What is the effective cost per dollar of inventory held on Polymarket vs Kalshi?** Polymarket capital sits in USDC.e on Polygon (gas + bridge friction); Kalshi sits in USD or USDC with Coinbase Custody. Cost-of-capital comparison is unpublished.
