# 1.72 Million Accounts, $13.76 Billion in Volume, 3.14% of Traders Doing the Work

A working paper on SSRN dated April 20, 2026 (revised five days later) by Roberto Gómez-Cram, Yunhan Guo, Theis Ingerslev Jensen and Howard Kung of London Business School and Yale [directly tested the foundational claim about prediction markets](https://www.coindesk.com/markets/2026/04/26/only-3-of-traders-drive-prediction-markets-accuracy-not-the-crowd-study-finds): that they aggregate the wisdom of crowds. The authors went through 1.72 million Polymarket accounts and $13.76 billion of volume. The crowd lost. Just 3.14% of accounts qualified as informed; the other 96.86% provided liquidity, paid the spread, and in aggregate funded the informed minority's gains.

The skill-versus-luck test was the part that should worry anyone with a short live-trading record. The authors simulated each trader's bets 10,000 times with the directions randomized to coin flips. [Only 12% of the biggest profit winners cleared that bootstrap benchmark](https://www.theblock.co/post/398902/skilled-polymarket-traders-are-a-3-minority-and-everyone-else-funds-their-gains-study). Roughly 60% of "lucky winners" — accounts with positive realized P&L on their actual sample — turned into losers when the same betting rule was tested against a separate sample of events. The shorter the live record, the larger that 60% gets.

## What "track record" has to clear before it counts

Quant finance has been here before. Marcos López de Prado and David H. Bailey's Minimum Backtest Length and [Deflated Sharpe Ratio](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=2460551) work formalized the math. Selection bias under multiple testing is a leading source of inflated reported Sharpe ratios; the deflated version corrects for the number of strategies tried and the non-Normality of the return stream. The practical implication is a tier system summarized in [BackTestBase's January 2026 calculator](https://www.backtestbase.com/education/how-many-trades-for-backtest): 30 trades is a Central-Limit-Theorem floor, not a guarantee; 100 is "basic reliability" of point metrics; 200 to 500 is the López-de-Prado institutional-grade band. The base formula for a 50% win-rate strategy at 95% confidence and 5% margin asks for 385 trades. An 80% win-rate strategy still wants 246.

What this collides with is the LP side of the conversation. Per Numerai's Richard Craib, [discussing JPMorgan's recent commitment of up to $500 million on AI Street](https://www.ai-street.co/p/the-hedge-fund-run-by-machines-is), institutional allocators ask for a three-year track record before they write a check. Numerai is six years old. The bar in practice is years, not trades.

## A research lab betting on a 100-trade gate, with the math against it

A small open-source research lab — the repo `tomscaria/swarm-fund-mvp`, with live numbers public at [rswarm.ai/metrics.json](https://rswarm.ai/metrics.json) — runs a four-state public lifecycle for every agent it ships: Birth → Canary → Apex → Revenant. Promotion from Canary to Apex requires 100 closed trades, Sharpe above 0.5, and a composite score above 0.5. The internal `AgentLifecycle` enum in `core/types.py` adds two branches the public surface doesn't show: `DEMOTED` for drawdown or regime mismatch, and `KILLED` for manual override.

The lab's furthest-along agent, CalibrationGap, sits at 29 closed trades — 76% win rate, +$415 P&L, Sharpe 0.31 against the gate's 0.5. By the LBS-Yale bootstrap, a 29-trade record is firmly inside the tier where the paper measured a 60% out-of-sample reversal rate. By the López-de-Prado tiering, 100 closed trades is exactly the boundary between "basic reliability" and the institutional-grade band — promotion at 100 hits the lower edge of that boundary, not the middle. A reasonable critic looking only at those numbers would say the gate is statistically theatrical: it clears the textbook minimum without clearing what the recent prediction-market evidence says is the actual bar.

## Where the lab is doing something the math doesn't price

The non-obvious detail is what gets generated *while* CalibrationGap is closing those next 71 trades. Every closed trade settles as a row in `metrics.json` on a public commit history. ADR-093 — commit `dc1846e`, May 3 — wired a polling adapter to a separate research repo: `python/execution/aeon_adapter.py` fetches `outputs/{skill}/{date}.json` from `tomscaria/aeon` every 15 minutes and publishes ticks with `kind="aeon_signal"` onto the same broker that handles Polymarket and Hyperliquid feeds. The same week, ADR-094 (commit `d010846`) shipped an LLM router that moved the `paper_triage` path from opus-4-7 to sonnet-4-6 with a thinking-token clamp — a cost-discipline change with its own commit hash and 28 new tests. Commit `1125deb` bumped the runner-swarm fleet from 74 to 112 agents using Latin-Hypercube sampling on a 10-dimensional factor space, with 30 of 38 net-new agents as variants of a single strategy.

That is not a track record in the LP sense. It is something neither the BackTestBase calculator nor the Deflated Sharpe Ratio paper measures: a public, content-addressed, replayable history of every agent decision and the cost of every model call that produced it. The numbers inside the gate are statistically thin. The audit trail underneath them — every demotion, every variant, every model downgrade visible in `git log` — is a property the institutional-grade-by-trade-count band does not even define.

## Forward claim

By Q4 2026 — once the EU AI Act's high-risk-system logging rules bite in August and the first wave of agentic-fund LP due-diligence cycles complete — expect at least one institutional allocator to publicly cite an auditable commit history, not Sharpe significance, as the binding pre-investment requirement for an AI-run book. The López-de-Prado tier system was built for backtests of human-designed strategies; it does not map cleanly onto agents that retrain themselves, ship new variants weekly, and emit cost reports per call. The next-generation gate will be the question: show me the git log of every decision your agent has made in production. A 100-trade promotion gate is, in that frame, the correct shape of the wrong measurement — a credibility ratchet, not an edge proof. If by November 2026 no top-50 allocator has cited audit-trail granularity in a public RFP or commitment letter, the framing here is wrong.
