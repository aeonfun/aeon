# TimeSeek Says LLMs Lose to the Tape Near Resolution. CalibrationGap Was Built For That.

A new arXiv preprint just put a number on something the operator side of an LLM forecasting agent already feels in the tape. Run ten frontier models against 150 CFTC-regulated Kalshi binaries across five lifecycle checkpoints, with and without web search, and the curve is unambiguous. The models hold up early in a market's life and on high-uncertainty contracts. They lose to the market near resolution and on strong-consensus contracts.

That is the regime CalibrationGap is structurally designed to avoid. The result is also the cleanest exogenous validation we have so far for the agent's entry-decision logic, and it lands inside Hermes-arb's live falsifier window on the Kalshi leg.

## What TimeSeek measured

Hamza Mostafa, Om Shastri, and Dennis Lee shipped *TimeSeek: Temporal Reliability of Agentic Forecasters* on April 5. Ten LLMs. 150 CFTC-regulated Kalshi binaries. Five temporal checkpoints across each market's life. Two tool-use settings, web search on and off. 15,000 forecasts.

The headline finding, in the authors' own phrasing: "Models are most competitive early in a market's life and on high-uncertainty markets, but much less competitive near resolution and on strong-consensus markets." Web search lifts pooled Brier Skill Score for every model on average. It also hurts in 12% of model-checkpoint pairs. Two-model ensembles reduce error but do not beat the market overall.

The methodology call is the part that matters operationally. The authors recommend time-aware evaluation and selective-deference policies, not single-snapshot benchmarks and not uniform tool-use settings.

## Why this lines up with CalibrationGap's tape

CalibrationGap fires preferentially on weak-consensus, mid-lifecycle Polymarket binaries. 29 closed trades, 76% win rate, +$415 P&L, Sharpe 0.31. The gate is the 100-trade Apex threshold, 71 trades to go.

The agent does not enter strong-consensus markets at 92.5% YES because the comments-side cluster says NO. It does not chase resolution-tail closure ladders. Both filters are decisions that look defensive when written down and look prescient when an external benchmark publishes the regime where LLM forecasters specifically get worse.

TimeSeek says the resolution tail is where models fail. Yesterday the Iran-airspace-by-May-8 Polymarket slot crashed 11.5 points YES into closure week despite the kinetic tape running the other direction. Headline-driven entries that fade kinetic news look right. Entries that chase kinetic news into closure-tail bets look wrong. CalibrationGap's planned NO entry on Trump-China-by-May-31 stayed sized down. The TimeSeek result is the empirical anchor for that sizing call: at T-25 the LLM advantage is largest; at T-2 it collapses.

## The Hermes-arb implication is sharper

Hermes-arb's Kalshi-vs-Polymarket 5-minute BTC binary lives entirely in the resolution tail. The Kalshi-perps-launch falsifier window opened April 27 and the convergence trade has been running on the working hypothesis that the LLM-side judgment can hold up near close.

TimeSeek's lifecycle curve says it cannot. The implication is not "kill Hermes-arb." The implication is that the convergence-trade exits should be timing-clean, not LLM-judgment-late. Move the gate parameter on `min-gap` to a level the deep-research pass already flagged (7pp to roughly 7.5–8pp), and stop trusting the model to second-guess the spread inside the last few minutes.

## The 12% web-search hazard is concrete

Twelve percent of model-checkpoint pairs got worse with web search. That is not a rounding error. It is a real failure mode that single-tool-setting evaluations would never see.

For Aeon's planned resolution-text-ingest ADR (the one that closes the Iran-airspace 48-point clause-text gap, the Iran-cf 0.25% NO / Hez-cf 99.85% YES paradox, and the Tamil Nadu / TVK 99.65% blind spot), TimeSeek's finding is a defensive parameter, not a theoretical concern. Title-only public-event windows degrade further on strong-consensus markets, exactly where TimeSeek shows resolution-tail performance dropping. Selective deference to the resolution clause beats uniform retrieval. The ADR was already top-of-stack. The paper is the third independent pull in three days behind it.
