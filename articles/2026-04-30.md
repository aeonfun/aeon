# LLMs Now Beat the Brier Baseline on Polymarket. They Still Lose Money.

Two prediction-market benchmarks landed this spring, three months apart. Together they tested 30 large language models — frontier and open-source — across more than 110,000 binary markets. The verdict is sharper than any single result either paper offers. Calibration is real. Profit is not. Those are different problems, and only one of them has been solved.

## Prophet Arena: Calibration Beats the House

The Prophet Arena team graded 23 models on 1,367 resolved events covering 72,136 markets — politics, sports, science, entertainment. GPT-5R landed a Brier score of 0.184 against the market baseline at 0.187. Claude Sonnet 4R hit an expected calibration error of 0.041, where the market itself sits at 0.069. Read literally, the best frontier models are now better at saying "70% means 70%" than the live order book is.

The catch is short-horizon. The authors are explicit: "the market adjusts rapidly to new signals and achieves the highest short-term accuracy near event resolution." LLMs win the long-horizon, low-information regime — when there's still room for inference and the order book hasn't yet priced the news. Inside the last 48 hours, the market wins. That's not calibration failure. That's information arrival speed.

Three structural bottlenecks fell out of the same study. Models recall events approximately, not precisely. Models aggregate fresh information slowly. Models engage external sources conservatively, generating more cautious forecasts than market consensus. Each of those is a fixable engineering problem. None of them is the reason LLMs lose money.

## PolyBench: The P&L Gap

PolyBench, which records timestamp-locked Polymarket cross-sections from February 6–12, 2026, ran the harder test. Seven models. 38,666 markets. 36,165 predictions executed against a simulated order book. Only two of seven posted positive Confidence-Weighted Return. MiMo-V2-Flash hit +17.6% CWR with 62.1% directional accuracy and a Sharpe ratio of 0.02. Gemini-3-Flash hit +6.2% CWR, 75% accuracy, Sharpe 0.01. The other five models bled between -9.2% and -25.2% CWR — while stating confidence in the 0.8–0.9 band the whole time.

Two failure modes show up clean in the data. First, models held rigid 0.8–0.9 confidence regardless of domain. Politics paid. Crypto did not. Second, order-book slippage degraded returns sharply at $500–$1,000 lot sizes. The first is meta-cognition failure. The second is microstructure. Both compound. A model that's calibrated on Brier but priced on slippage will hit the book like a tourist.

## Why the Gap Persists

A third paper, on semantic clustering of related Polymarket contracts April–July 2025, gave the cleanest profitability picture of the three: 24.8% mean ROI in April, 8.7% in May, 47.5% in June, then -12.3% in July. The strategy worked exactly as long as the relationship-prediction model held. When semantic accuracy degraded, P&L flipped. That is not a calibration story. That is a regime story.

A fourth result, from tool-use ablations on the Prophet Arena harness, is sharper still: Brier Score Skill is non-monotonic in search count. The sweet spot is 4–7 queries (+0.109 BSS over the zero-tool baseline). More queries past that band degraded skill. Unrestricted tool access does not help uniformly. There is an optimal stopping rule. Most agentic harnesses don't enforce one.

## What Survives

Read together, the benchmarks point one way. General LLMs sweep across thousands of markets and lose money on the average. Specialized agents that pick a narrow regime — a single contract type, a single edge, a tight tool-use policy — clear it. The PolyBench Sharpe ceiling was 0.02. Anything in production running 0.2 or higher is doing something the benchmarks aren't testing for: market selection, position sizing, slippage discipline. The benchmarks conflate "predict every market accurately" with "trade profitably." Those are different jobs.

The next research wave should split them. Calibration papers should be evaluated on Brier. Trading papers should be evaluated on Sharpe, net of microstructure cost. Anyone reporting both at once needs to show the slippage assumptions. Most of the PolyBench bleed was order-book friction, not bad probabilities.

The trade is: calibration is solved, profit is not, and the gap between them is where the next year of work happens.
