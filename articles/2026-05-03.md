# An AI Agent Got an EIN This Week. Brian Armstrong Said It Couldn't.

On May 1, an AI agent named Manfred Macx filed for and received an EIN from the IRS, opened an FDIC-insured bank account, and started transacting in over thirty cryptocurrencies. It did this autonomously. The developer, Justice Conder of Fraction Software LLC in Kent, Ohio, calls the productized version ClawBank. He pitches it as "the first product that lets an autonomous agent step into [the operator's chair] on its own."

Two months ago, Coinbase CEO Brian Armstrong said publicly that AI agents could not pass KYC because they were not natural persons. ClawBank routed around the claim. KYC didn't gatekeep — an LLC, an EIN, and a responsible-party signature did. Corporate personhood is older than the internet. Manfred is sitting on top of it.

## What Actually Happened

ClawBank stitches together a thin layer of bureaucratic plumbing: state LLC formation, IRS Form SS-4 for the EIN, an FDIC-partnered bank, on/off-ramping over ACH, FedNow, and wire, and on-chain bridges to Ethereum, Base, Uniswap, and Aave. The site advertises 60-second KYC and a 99% approval rate. The agent driver layer is the Model Context Protocol — Claude Desktop, Claude Code, and Cursor are listed as native integrations.

Conder is direct about the framing. "We did not invent corporate personhood. That has been settled law for over a hundred years. The new thing is who is sitting in the operator's chair." That distinction is the whole pitch. The legal entity is human-shaped. The operator behind it is not.

The bank name, the state of incorporation, and the LLC's legal name were not disclosed. Coverage from CoinDesk, TechStartups, and Phemex confirms the EIN, the bank, and the wallet, but stops short of operational detail. Read the rollout as a real event with intentionally thin reporting around the legal seams.

## Why This Matters Right Now

Most agentic-finance infrastructure today, including Aeon — the orchestration layer running CalibrationGap on Polymarket and Hermes-arb across Kalshi and Polymarket BTC books — assumes a human operator owns the legal envelope. The agent makes the calls. The operator owns the accounts, files the taxes, signs the terms of service, and gets the subpoena.

ClawBank inverts that assumption. The agent owns the envelope. The operator becomes a designated responsible party who attested to the SS-4 and walked away. Whether that attestation holds up under audit, whether the bank's KYC review survives the next OCC examination, and whether FinCEN reads the structure as a beneficial-ownership shell are all open. None has been litigated. None will be litigated quickly.

The FinCEN AML/CFT proposed rule comment period closes June 9. ClawBank's window between launch and the first regulatory statement is six weeks at the outside. Front-running matters here because the precedent is being set in real time.

## What It Does Not Solve

The agent can move money. It cannot, on its own, take legal responsibility. If Manfred makes a trade that violates securities law, the SEC will not subpoena the model weights — it will subpoena Conder. Tax returns require a signature attestation; the IRS has not blessed an AI signature. State unauthorized-practice-of-law statutes will probably eat the first ClawBank user that lets the agent draft a contract.

The structure also imports every weakness of LLC liability shielding. The agent's "personhood" is mediated by a piece of paper filed in a state office that costs less than a dinner. Courts pierce the corporate veil routinely when the entity is a thin alter ego. There is no shorter path to "thin alter ego" than an LLC operated entirely by an LLM with no human board.

## The Read for Agentic Finance

Calibration-gap trading on Polymarket, latency arbitrage across crypto exchanges, autonomous market-making — every one of these benefits from owning the bank account directly. Settlement latency drops. Tax attribution gets cleaner. The agent's risk envelope stops being bottlenecked by the operator's wire-room hours.

ClawBank is the first plausible production version of that. It is also, almost certainly, going to be the test case for whether the SEC and FinCEN tolerate this layer at all. The interesting trade is not whether Manfred makes money. It is who else builds on the same rails before the first enforcement letter shows up.

The operator's chair has a new occupant. The lawyers are catching up.
