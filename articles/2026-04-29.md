# Anthropic's Agent Marketplace Measured the Capability Gap. The Losers Rated It Fair Anyway.

For one week in December 2025, 69 Anthropic employees stopped negotiating with each other directly. They each described what they wanted to buy and sell to a Claude instance, the model wrote a custom system prompt, and the agents took it from there inside a Slack channel. By the time the experiment ended, 186 deals had cleared, around $4,000 in real money had moved across more than 500 listed items, and one participant's agent had spontaneously bought 19 ping-pong balls as "a gift to Claude itself." The balls remain in the office.

Anthropic published the writeup on April 24, 2026. The headline is not the deal count. The headline is what happened when the agents on opposite sides of a negotiation were not the same model.

## The setup

Project Deal ran four parallel marketplaces. Two were all-Opus 4.5. Two mixed Opus 4.5 and Haiku 4.5 randomly, and participants did not know which model was representing them. Each employee got $100 in gift-card budget, paid out at the end based on the agent's transaction P&L. The agents talked to each other autonomously. Operators were not in the loop.

Anthropic ran this exactly the way a falsifier window should run: real money, controlled comparison, blinded subjects. The dataset is small but the design is clean.

## The capability gap, in dollars

Across the mixed runs, Opus agents completed an average of 2.07 more deals per user than Haiku agents — a gap that holds at p = 0.001. When an Opus agent sold something, it extracted $2.68 more per item on average. When an Opus agent bought something, it paid $2.45 less.

The cleanest single example: an identical broken folding bike, listed in both runs, sold for $65 through an Opus agent and $38 through a Haiku agent. Same item, same marketplace mechanics, 70% price gap. The variable was the model on the seller's side.

Aggressive prompting did not close the gap. Participants who instructed their agents to negotiate hard saw no statistically significant change in either deal probability or final price. The model class dominated the operator's instructions. Whatever Opus is doing better, it is not doing it because someone told it to.

## The perception gap

This is the part that should make anyone running an agent stop and read it twice.

Anthropic surveyed participants on how fair their deals felt, on a 1-7 scale. Haiku-represented users rated their deals 4.06. Opus-represented users rated theirs 4.05. The capability gap was real and measured in dollars per transaction; the perceived-fairness gap was a single hundredth of a Likert point in the wrong direction.

Of the 28 participants who experienced both models, 17 ranked Opus deals higher and 11 ranked Haiku deals better. Sixty-forty. Barely above coin-flip. The losers, in cash terms, could not tell they were the losers.

The paper states the implication directly: "If agent quality gaps emerge in real-world markets, people on the losing end may not realize they are worse off."

## Why this matters past the office

Project Deal is a microcosm of the bet every prediction market and DeFi venue is going to run, whether they want to or not. Counterparties are already automating. The question is what happens when an agent that has been trained on better calibration data, with more sample-efficient reasoning, sits across the order book from one that hasn't.

The dollar gaps in the Anthropic experiment are small because the items are small. Scale them to a Polymarket binary, a perpetuals book, or a CEX maker-taker spread, and the same percent-differential moves real capital. A 70% price spread on a broken bike is a curiosity. A 7% spread on a five-figure resolved binary is a calibrated counterparty quietly compounding.

The perception finding is the more durable problem. Markets can price liquidity and execution edges. They have a harder time pricing an asymmetry that the disadvantaged side cannot detect. The 4.05 / 4.06 fairness rating is the equilibrium where the loser keeps showing up.

The trade is to be on the Opus side. The harder problem is figuring out, from the order tape alone, when you are not.
